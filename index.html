Aggressive web synthesis, then design

How CAR-T therapies work, where they break, and where the field is moving.

CAR-T is not just a drug. It is a manufactured immune program: collect T cells, rewrite them to recognize a tumor antigen, expand them outside the body, then send them back as a living cytotoxic population that can proliferate, persist, and sometimes clear disease for years. This page locks the landscape to March 23, 2026, separates stable biology from time-sensitive trial activity, and focuses on what matters mechanistically.

The Mechanism

The CAR is a modular receptor mounted onto a T cell. Commercial CAR-T products still mostly use second-generation constructs: an extracellular binder, a transmembrane segment, one costimulatory domain, and CD3z signaling. What follows is a staged immune program, not a single pharmacologic event.

Blueprint of a CAR

The extracellular region recognizes a surface antigen on the target cell. The intracellular region converts that binding event into T-cell activation, proliferation, cytokine secretion, and cytotoxicity. NCI's overview highlights the same broad architecture across approved products, even though specific binders and signaling modules differ.

How the sequence actually unfolds

Commercial CAR-T is best understood as a staged lifecycle: manufacturing, conditioning, in vivo expansion, tumor killing, and persistence. The first two steps happen before the infused cells ever touch a cancer cell.

1
Leukapheresis and manufacturing T cells are collected from the patient, genetically modified ex vivo to express a CAR, then expanded. NCI describes the current commercial interval from collection to infusion as roughly 3 to 5 weeks.
2
Lymphodepletion Fludarabine/cyclophosphamide regimens are commonly used before infusion to make physiologic space for the incoming cells and support early expansion.
3
Antigen recognition The engineered receptor binds a tumor-surface antigen directly without MHC restriction, one reason CAR-T works especially well in B-cell malignancies.
4
Immune synapse and signaling Binding initiates CD3z phosphorylation, ZAP-70 recruitment, LAT signaling, and transcriptional programs such as NFAT, NF-kB, and AP-1, which collectively drive activation, proliferation, and effector function.
5
Cytotoxic execution Activated cells form a productive synapse and kill target cells through perforin/granzyme release, additional death-receptor signaling, and cytokine-mediated amplification of immunity.
6
Expansion, contraction, persistence Clinical kinetics are multiphasic: distribution, rapid expansion, contraction, then a persistence phase. Modeling and translational studies place the expansion peak at roughly the first two weeks in many patients.

Pressure Points

The field now understands that CAR-T outcomes are shaped by a tension between immediate potency, long-term persistence, antigen stability, and the hostility of the tumor environment. This is why next-generation programs keep returning to the same design themes: dual targeting, armored constructs, genome editing, and off-the-shelf manufacturing.

Potency vs persistence

A 2026 review comparing CD28- and 4-1BB-based CD19 CAR-T products describes a recurring pattern: CD28 tends toward faster expansion and stronger initial punch, while 4-1BB tends toward deeper persistence and longer memory-like behavior.

CD28-biased profile

rapid response

Useful when fast expansion matters; may trade some durability for early intensity.

4-1BB-biased profile

longer persistence

Often associated with slower but more durable persistence across current commercial designs.

Why remission fails

Resistance is now understood as a layered systems problem, not a single escape route. Antigen loss is central in CD19 disease, but CAR-T dysfunction and tumor ecology matter just as much.

Antigen escape

Tumor cells can lose or downregulate CD19 or evolve heterogeneous target expression, which directly weakens target engagement.

T-cell exhaustion

Poor starting material, chronic stimulation, and suppressive signaling can produce less durable, less proliferative cell products.

Microenvironmental blockade

Hypoxia, nutrient deprivation, TGF-beta, IL-10, suppressive myeloid cells, and checkpoint signaling all inhibit function.

Trafficking failure

Solid tumors remain hard because the cells must reach the lesion, persist there, and still find a safe antigen once they arrive.

Safety watch, March 2026 CRS and ICANS remain the headline toxicities, but prolonged cytopenias, infection risk, hypogammaglobulinemia, and on-target B-cell depletion still matter clinically. FDA also maintains a class-level concern about rare post-treatment T-cell malignancies in autologous BCMA- and CD19-directed CAR-T, and the CARVYKTI page shows the 2025 boxed-warning update for immune effector cell-associated enterocolitis.

US Approved Landscape

The FDA product roster current through late February and March 2026 shows a still concentrated market: approved CAR-T remains a hematologic modality, dominated by CD19 and BCMA. The key 2025 shift was expansion of BREYANZI into marginal zone lymphoma.

Approval arc

Product approvals began in 2017 and expanded steadily across leukemias, aggressive lymphomas, follicular lymphoma, mantle cell lymphoma, CLL/SLL, multiple myeloma, and now marginal zone lymphoma.

Trial Horizon

The ongoing pipeline is no longer just "more CD19." The design patterns are clear: reduce antigen escape with dual targets, resist checkpoint suppression with armored constructs, move faster with allogeneic platforms, and finally crack solid tumors without intolerable off-tumor damage.
